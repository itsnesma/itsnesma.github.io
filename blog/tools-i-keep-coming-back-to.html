Tools I keep coming back to

A short list of software that has earned its place on every machine I own.

I download a lot of software. Most of it I delete within a week. A small handful I install on every new machine without thinking. This is that list, and a few words on why each one stuck.

I'm leaving out the obvious ones — the browser, the editor, the terminal. Those are tools you already have opinions about. These are the ones I rarely see on other people's lists, but I'd struggle to work without.

The list

A clipboard manager

I won't name a specific one because they're all roughly equivalent — what matters is that you have one. Once you've copied your tenth thing of the day and lost the eighth, you understand. The cost is zero, the upside is enormous, and the people who don't have one are operating with a real handicap they don't know about.

A keyboard launcher

Spotlight, Raycast, Alfred, whatever. The ability to open any app, file, or action without lifting your hands from the keyboard is the single biggest productivity multiplier I've ever found. It's also the only one I'd describe as "feels like cheating."

A daily plain-text file

The best note-taking app is whatever app you'll actually open tomorrow.

Mine is a folder full of YYYY-MM-DD.md files. No tags, no categories, no fancy linking. Just whatever I was thinking that day, plus a running to-do at the top. I have years of these now and they're more useful than any of the dozen specialized note apps I've tried and abandoned.

A second monitor — vertical

Not for code. For everything that's tall: documents, terminals, conversations, web pages. The horizontal monitor is for the work; the vertical one is for the context around the work. Try it for a week. You won't go back.

What I've stopped using

For balance, here's what I've quietly given up on: every app that promised to organize my thinking, every "second brain" that turned into a first chore, every habit tracker that lasted longer than the habit. The pattern is that tools meant to do my thinking for me eventually feel like a tax on the thinking.

The tools that stick are the ones that get out of the way.
