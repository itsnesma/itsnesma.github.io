Why I rebuilt my personal site (again)

On the eternal cycle of redesigning the one thing nobody asked you to redesign.

Every developer I know has a personal site. Most of them are unfinished. Mine has been "almost done" for about six years now, and every eighteen months I tear it down and start over. This is the story of why, and what I learned the latest time around.

The honest answer is that personal sites are never really about communicating information. They're about working out what you think — about design, about yourself, about the kind of person you want to be on the internet. Mine started as a portfolio, became a blog, became a links page, became a portfolio again, and somewhere along the way I forgot what I was even trying to say.

The trap of the perfect site

Every time I sit down to redesign, I get stuck on the same thing: what is this for? I want it to do too many jobs. Be a portfolio. Show personality. Host writing. Look impressive at first glance and reward repeat visits. The result is usually nothing — or worse, a site so dense with options that visitors bounce in three seconds.

The personal site is a self-portrait. The hardest part isn't the code, it's deciding which version of yourself shows up.

This time I tried something different: I picked one feeling I wanted people to leave with, and built backwards from there. Everything that didn't serve that feeling got cut. It was painful. I think it worked.

What I changed

A few specific things, if you're curious:

  • Killed the dropdown menus. Five flat links beats a tree.
  • Picked a font with personality and committed to it everywhere.
  • Added a small piece of unnecessary motion — character heads, in my case — because I wanted the site to feel like someone made it, not like a template.
  • Wrote actual paragraphs about projects instead of bullet points. Bullet points are where curiosity goes to die.

What I'd tell past me

Ship the ugly version. The site you have at 70% looks better to visitors than the perfect site you'll never finish. Iterate in public. The third redesign is always better than the first, and the only way to get to the third is to publish the first.

I'll probably redesign this again in 2027. That's fine. The site is a self-portrait, and self-portraits change.
