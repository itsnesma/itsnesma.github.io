Notes on making interfaces feel alive

On animation, character, and the small details that turn software into something you actually want to use.

The best interfaces feel like they're paying attention. Not in a creepy, surveillance way — in the way a good barista clocks that you're a regular before you've finished saying your order. There's a presence to them. They notice you, and you notice that they notice.

Most software doesn't feel like that. Most software feels like a vending machine: you put in input, it returns output, and the only feedback you get is a vague mechanical hum. This is a choice, and we made it, and we can unmake it.

Three small things

I keep a running list of moments where an interface made me smile. They're almost never the big features. They're the small ones — the cursor that wiggles when you hover the wrong place, the loading state that uses the time to tell you a joke, the empty state that admits it's empty.

Personality in software is rarely a feature. It's almost always a thousand tiny choices that nobody would have asked you to make.

Three patterns I steal from constantly:

  • Acknowledged actions. When I click something, the interface should acknowledge it before the result arrives. Even ten milliseconds of feedback prevents the click-twice spiral.
  • Earned animation. Animation isn't decoration. It's a way of saying "I noticed what you did." Use it where it carries meaning, skip it where it doesn't.
  • Honest empty states. An empty inbox doesn't need to pretend it's full. Tell me there's nothing here, and tell me what could go here.

The risk

The risk with all of this is that it tips into cute. There's a fine line between "an interface with personality" and "an interface that won't shut up." Cute interfaces age badly. Functional interfaces with one or two unexpected moments age beautifully.

The test I use: would I still want this on my site in three years? If yes, keep it. If no, it's probably cute and probably has to go.
